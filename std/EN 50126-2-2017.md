**Introduction**


EN 50126-1:1999 was aiming at introducing the application of a systematic RAMS management process
in the railway sector. Through the application of this standard and the experiences gained over the last
years, the need for revision and restructuring became apparent with a need to deliver a systematic and
coherent approach to RAMS applicable to all the railway application fields Command, Control and
Signalling, Rolling Stock and Fixed Installations.

The revision work improved the coherency and consistency of the standards, the concept of safety
[management and the practical usage of EN 50126 and took into consideration the existing and related](http://dx.doi.org/10.3403/01922440U)
Technical Reports as well.

This European Standard provides railway duty holders and the railway suppliers, throughout the European
Union, with a process which will enable the implementation of a consistent approach to the management
of reliability, availability, maintainability and safety, denoted by the acronym RAMS.

Processes for the specification and demonstration of RAMS requirements are cornerstones of this
standard. This European Standard promotes a common understanding and approach to the management
of RAMS.

[EN 50126 forms part of the railway sector specific application of IEC 61508. Meeting the requirements in](http://dx.doi.org/10.3403/01922440U)
this European Standard together with the requirements of other suitable standards is sufficient to ensure
that additional compliance to IEC 61508 does not need to be demonstrated.

With regard to safety, EN 50126-1 provides a Safety Management Process which is supported by guidance
and methods described in EN 50126-2.

EN 50126-1 and EN 50126-2 are independent from the technology used. As far as safety is concerned,
[EN 50126 takes the perspective of safety with a functional approach.](http://dx.doi.org/10.3403/01922440U)

The application of this standard should be adapted to the specific requirements for the system under
consideration.

This European Standard can be applied systematically by the railway duty holders and railway suppliers,
throughout all phases of the life-cycle of a railway application, to develop railway specific RAMS
requirements and to achieve compliance with these requirements. The systems-level approach developed
by this European Standard facilitates assessment of the RAMS interactions between elements of railway
applications even if they are of complex nature.

This European Standard promotes co-operation between the stakeholders of Railways in the achievement
of an optimal combination of RAMS and cost for railway applications. Adoption of this European Standard
will support the principles of the European Single Market and facilitate European railway inter-operability.

In accordance with CENELEC editing rules [1)], mandatory requirements in this standard are indicated with
the modal verb “shall”. Where justifiable, the standard permits process tailoring.

Specific guidance on the application of this standard for Safety aspects is provided in EN 50126-2.
EN 50126-2 provides various methods for use in the safety management process. Where a particular
method is selected for the system under consideration, the mandatory requirements of this method are by
consequence mandatory for the safety management of the system under consideration.

This European Standard consists of the main part (Clause 1 to Clause 11) and Annexes A, B, C, D, E, F,
G and ZZ. The requirements defined in the main part of the standard are normative, whilst Annexes are
informative.

**5** **Safety process**


**5.1 Risk assessment and hazard control**


In this sub-clause, the Hourglass Model is introduced: it offers a simplified approach that, although not
containing all aspects implied in the life-cycle model, helps to clarify some issues.

The Hourglass Model provides an overview of the major safety-related activities that are needed to ensure
an acceptable safety level for a technical system, including the corresponding responsibility areas.

Technical system means a product or an assembly of products including the design, implementation and
support documentation. The development of a technical system starts with its requirements specification
and ends with its acceptance. The design of relevant interfaces considering interactions with human
operators and their behaviour is considered, while human operators themselves and their actions are not
included in a technical system. Both the maintenance process (described in the maintenance manuals) and
the operation are specified but are not considered parts of the technical system itself. They can be restricted
by “application conditions”.

The purpose of this model is to highlight the separation between risk analysis as part of risk assessment
(at the railway system level) from hazard analysis as part of hazard control (at the level of the system under
consideration).

This enhances co-operation between the relevant stakeholders, clarifying responsibilities and interfaces
and has the advantages of reducing complexity and facilitating modularization.

The Hourglass Model describes two main aspects:

- risk assessment, deriving safety requirements for operational and technical issues (including
maintenance), and


9


- hazard control, satisfying given functional safety requirements coming from upper levels by
determining and analysing causes and designing and implementing control measures.

**5.2 A. Risk assessment**


**5.2.1** **General**


Risk assessment is performed at the railway system level.

It relies on a System Definition and includes Risk analysis and Risk evaluation.

It defines the high level system safety requirements, in particular safety requirements for the system under
consideration from the perspective of the railway duty holder and the operator. It takes into account safetyrelated operational aspects, previous experience and the regulatory requirements for the railway
application.

The main task for this activity is the risk analysis, which is derived from the system definition. The risk
analysis includes hazard identification, consequence analysis, and selection of Risk Acceptance Principle
(RAP).


10


The specification of safety requirements is the final result of risk assessment; in Figure 1 it is allocated to
box B, because it constitutes an interface (together with system requirement specifications and the list of
identified hazards) between different responsibilities.

**5.2.2** **Conducting risk assessment**


The level of detail in a risk assessment should be adequate to enable the risk to be properly considered.
The purpose is not to catalogue every trivial hazard, nor is it expected that hazards beyond the limits of
current knowledge will always be identified. A risk assessment should reflect a reasonable analysis of
hazards and their associated risks within the railway operation and within the applied technology itself.
Where considered to be worthwhile, risk assessments should be correlated with historical records of
accidents and the records of causes.

When possible, consideration of technical implementation/architecture should be avoided in this first stage,
i.e. the system to be developed should be considered as a black box, of which functions and hazards are
evaluated only at the boundaries. These boundaries are well defined interfaces between the operational
environment and the system under consideration.

As an example, an “unintentional train motion” is a hazard for a train. It can be observed as an abstraction
at the boundary of the “system train” and it could lead to different accidents depending on the operational
context (e.g. collision in context with over-speeding while running or fall of persons in connection with a
train moving in a station while expected to stand still, etc.).

Assumptions defined during the risk assessment shall be checked and updated throughout the life-cycle
phases.

**5.3 B. Outcome of the risk assessment**


The results of the risk assessment are a set of safety requirements associated to clearly-identified functions,
systems or operating rules. They are part of the System Requirement Specification which establishes the
technical interface between the stakeholders.

NOTE The project organisational structure and responsibilities are other factors to consider in understanding and
controlling risk. For organisational aspects and requirements it is advised to refer to Clause 7.


On the basis of the selected risk acceptance principles, safety requirements can refer to Codes of Practice,
to Reference Systems, or give explicit targets derived from an Explicit Risk Estimation (ERE).

Safety requirements include required safety functions, which could be assessed quantitatively (e.g.
maximum rates of hazards), semi-quantitatively or qualitatively (e.g. use of trained drivers for controlling
human errors).

Safety requirements should be assessed with an holistic approach to the system under consideration, i.e.
the residual risk of the whole system after introducing safety requirements should be assessed taking into
consideration all identified hazards.

**5.4 C. Hazard control**


The hazard control stage in the hourglass model ensures that the system under consideration is compliant
with the safety requirements. Hazard control is performed for a specific system architecture.

The major impacts of human factors, operational and general maintenance rules as well as procedures are
part of the preceding risk analysis and should have already been taken into account in the safety
requirements. Therefore, during hazard control, the designer of the system under consideration can focus
on the internal causes of the identified hazards.

The main task is the “hazard analysis” comprising:

- a dedicated hazard identification focusing on the system under consideration (refinement);


- causal analysis;


- a Common Cause Analysis (see 11.4 for details).


11


Hazard identification is a recurring task, iterating on several levels during development of the system under
consideration. In order to distinguish between different tasks (and related documents) the hazard
identification has been quoted twice in Figure 1:

1. during risk assessment, hazard identification focuses on high level hazards derived from the system
functions (black box) and related operation of the system as well as its environment;


2. within the hazard control, a refined/iterated hazard identification focuses on hazards and their causes
derived from the technical solutions, i.e. from defined architecture and internal interfaces of the system
under consideration, and potential new hazards introduced by the system itself.


Both kinds of identified hazards shall be addressed during hazard control. Figure 2 shows the general case
where the cause of a hazard at the railway system level consists of a hazard on the level of the system
under consideration, with respect to its boundary. The boundary for a hazard identification is always given
in the system definition that limits the scope of the task. This implies that the hazards are structured
hierarchically. Hence a hierarchical approach to hazard analysis and hazard logging should be used.

**5.5 D. Revision of risk assessment**


During the hazard control stage, fulfilment of safety requirements might not be reached in the first iteration.
Three potential causes are:

- additional hazards are identified at the level of the system under consideration;


- a need for new operational rules arises;


- additional external safety measures are required to fulfil the safety targets.


In all these cases, a revision of the risk assessment is necessary.


12


This revision should also take into account the application conditions that could arise at the level of the
system under consideration.

**5.6 Responsibilities**


Risk assessment is mainly within the responsibility of the railway duty holders and operators. If no risk
assessment is provided by the railway duty holder or operator, the roles and responsibilities may be
contracted to other parties (manufacturers and suppliers), provided that they have a documented and
suitable range of competencies to consider the whole operational context in detail. They shall assess the
risk resulting from introducing changes to the operational context, taking into account safety-related
operational aspects, previous experience and regulatory requirements. In any case the railway duty holders
should accept the results of the risk assessment.

The supplier of the technical system is responsible for the hazard control. In the case that several suppliers
are in charge for different systems under consideration, the railway duty holder shall be responsible for
organising an overall hazard control.

Railway duty holder and supplier shall conform to the prevailing legal requirements.


**6** **Safety demonstration and acceptance**


**6.1 Introduction**


This clause provides additional details on safety demonstration and safety acceptance processes for the
system under consideration. Except where considered appropriate, it does not specify who should carry
out the work at each stage, since this may vary in different circumstances.

Safety demonstration evidence is based on the safety case. The purpose and content of the safety case
are defined in EN 50126-1:2017, Clause 8.

In terms of safety processes, the development of a system can be categorised in three types:

- Generic Product: The system is considered from a generic point of view, applicable to different classes
of applications;


Analyses are carried out within an operational context which is application-independent.


- Generic Application: The system is considered suitable for multiple applications of the same class;


Analyses are carried out within an operational context which is application-dependent. The safety
process includes the definition of the application design process.


- Specific application: The system is considered for a specific application (including its physical
implementation).


**6.2 Safety demonstration and safety acceptance process**


Three different categories of safety case can be defined according to the involved type of development as
previously defined:

**6.3 Responsibility in managing the Safety Case**


Responsibility for managing the safety case depends on the defined relationship between stakeholders and
it is subjected to contractual agreements. Typically:

- The Generic Product Safety Case is the evidence written by the supplier for the party responsible for
configuring it for use (e.g. a supplier or contracting entity).


- The Generic Application Safety Case is the evidence written by the party responsible for generic
configuration of the product (e.g. the product supplier) for the party responsible for specific
configuration (e.g. the supplier, a contracting entity, or RU/IM project delivery organisation).


- The Specific Application Safety Case is the evidence written by the party configuring the system for
specific use (e.g. the supplier or a contracting entity or RU/IM project delivery organisation) for the
party responsible for the operational railway (e.g. the RU or IM).


As the work progresses from generic to specific arrangements, the safety argument evolves and builds
upon each previous safety case, and the ultimate specific application safety case therefore comprises
evidence from all related safety cases. Safety-Related Application Conditions are developed at each stage
and taken forward by the next actor. By definition, the safety case for a specific application, and the safetyrelated application conditions it defines, once accepted by the RU or IM, become an extension of the safety
management system of the RU or IM. At that level, all the SRACs are evaluated to have been met.

**6.4 Modifications after safety acceptance**


After a system/sub-system/component has received safety acceptance, any subsequent modification shall
be controlled using the same quality management, safety management and functional/technical safety
criteria as would be used for a new design. All relevant documentation, including the Safety Case, shall be
updated or supplemented by additional documentation, and the modified design should be subject to the
acceptance processes in the duty holder's safety management system and the approval processes required
by the particular legal framework.

Once an installed system/sub-system/component has been validated and accepted, appropriate
procedures, support systems and safety monitoring, as defined in the Safety Plan, are used to ensure
continued safe operation throughout its working life, including operation, maintenance, alteration, extension
and eventual decommissioning. These activities are controlled using quality management, safety
management and technical safety criteria at least as safe as for the original design. The on-going safety of
the system is managed by the duty holder and this should require the relevant documentation, including
the Safety Case, to be kept up-to-date.

**6.5 Dependencies between Safety Cases**


The safety case for a system could depend on the safety cases of other sub-systems or equipment. In such
circumstances, safety acceptance of the main system is not possible without previous safety acceptance
of the related sub-systems/equipment.

Relationship among safety cases shall be associated to a corresponding system decomposition established
during life-cycle phase 2 “system definition and operational context”.

The different types of Safety Case may be defined according to reuse needs.

If independent safety assessment report has been obtained for a generic product, or for a generic
application, a reference may be made to this for safety acceptance of a specific application; it is not needed
to repeat the generic independent safety assessment process for each specific application. This
dependency between Safety Cases is illustrated with examples in Figure 4.

A safety case may be based on demonstration that the proposed specific application is technically
equivalent to an existing application with specific safety acceptance. A new safety acceptance for this
specific application is necessary.

It is essential to ensure that the Safety-Related Application Conditions (SRACs) stated in the Technical
Safety Report of each Safety Case are fulfilled in the higher-level Safety Case, or else are carried forward
into the SRACs of the higher-level Safety Case.

**6.6 Relationship between safety cases and system architecture**


It is not necessary for the number, scope and hierarchy of the various safety cases (GPSC, GASC, SASC)
to reflect the architectural view of the system under development, i.e. “generic product” (GP), “generic
application” (GA) and “specific application” (SA). The safety case architecture should not be confused with
“system”, “sub-system” and “component” architectural view.

Although a GP is often a component in a GA or SA, those terms could even refer to the same item, just
with different perspectives.

EXAMPLE A component implementing a safety-related communication over a transmission system, to exchange
the status of a field equipment:


a) [It can be considered as a GP, and validated / approved with reference to EN 50159, if its scope is the mitigation](http://dx.doi.org/10.3403/30202175U)
of generic threats to messages (deletion, corruption, re-sequencing, etc.), regardless of their content. A number
of safety-related parameters will be defined as configurable (e.g., transmission cycle, length of time-outs, number
of replies);


b) It can be considered as a GA, and validated / approved with reference to a set of applications requirements. Type
of field equipment, its dynamics and how the status information is used will define all the safety-related parameters
configurable at the GP level;


c) It can be considered as a SA for a specific site, and validated for its specific use, within the parameters’ ranges
defined as application conditions in the GA.


These steps might be grouped according to the needs of re-use, e.g. step a) might be useful if the system is intended
to be used for completly different applications, otherwise the related safety analyses could be integrated in b). Similarly,
step b) is advisable if the system will be replicated on different physical installations.

**8** **Risk assessment**


**8.1 Introduction**


The core part of the Risk assessment (i.e. analysis and evaluation) is performed at phase 3 of the life-cycle
and continued as appropriate through the system life-cycle. This clause describes:

- Sub-clause 8.2: Risk analysis with identification of the hazards and their consequences;

- Sub-clause 8.3: Selection of risk acceptance principles, related acceptance criteria and risk
evaluation. Risk acceptance principles are;

- Use of Code of Practice;


- Use of a reference system;


- Use of Explicit Risk Estimation.


- Sub-clause 8.4: Application of explicit risk estimation


The risk assessment process can be described as several tasks such as Risk Analysis, Hazard
Identification, Consequence Analysis, Risk Evaluation, Selection of Risk Acceptance Principles.

**8.2 Risk Analysis**


**8.2.1** **General**


Initially, the analysis is performed qualitatively, identifying all contributing factors and their combination
without quantification.

The risk analysis process is an iterative process. It shall start with the definition of the system under
consideration. Then the hazards, the risks, the associated safety measures, and the resulting safety
requirements for the system under consideration shall be identified.

Consequence analysis, see 8.2.3, involves gathering and documenting data that describe the effects of a
hazard. The recommended approach is to use accident data, wherever available, and/or to consult experts
on the environment related to the system or process under consideration (i.e. “domain experts”).

**8.2.2** **The risk model**


Where needed to support hazard identification, hazard classification and the selected risk acceptance
principles a risk model should be defined. Causes, hazards and accidents stand in a many-to-many
relationship, such as:

- a single cause can trigger (or contribute to) several different hazards;


- a hazard can be triggered by several different causes;


- a hazard can result in different types of accidents under different operational and environmental
contexts;


- an accident can have different consequences under different operational and environmental contexts.


An example of risk model is provided in Figure 7 and shows:


22


- how a hazard of the (sub)system under consideration, due to technical and operational circumstances,
can develop into a hazard at the railway system level. Thus it can lead to an accident according to the
model in Figure 2, based on triggering events and external barriers, if any;


- how a hazard at the railway system level can develop under a particular operational and environmental
context into an accident with a specific set of consequences.


Such a model could, in a qualitative and flexible way, identify the links between hazards, triggering events
and barriers in a given accident scenario.

In specific cases hazards at the level of the system under consideration could directly lead to hazards at
the railway system level, and these could directly develop into accidents. In other cases barriers might be
effective.

hazard at the level of the system under consideration can develop into a hazard at the railway system level
which might cause an accident. A hazard can develop into a different potential accident for different
operational and/or environmental contexts. Operational and environmental context should also include all
the operational or environmental conditions that are necessary for the understanding of the accident
scenario.

EXAMPLE 1 An operational context can consist of a train in normal operation, at standstill in a station, with a

- operational causes (wrong operation or maintenance) and

The hazard at the railway system level is a hazard that affects a railway in operation as a comprehensive

EXAMPLE 4 For a collision at low speed, the external barrier reducing severity could be a passive energy absorbing

**8.2.3** **Techniques for the consequence analysis**


Due to the complexity of the scenarios under analysis, the consequence analysis is performed using
bottom-up techniques or a combination of bottom-up and top-down techniques.

The role of expert judgement is fundamental for the analysis and the calibration of the techniques used in
support.

Different techniques can be used, often in combination, to perform the consequence analysis. Commonly
used techniques are briefly listed:

- Event Tree Analysis


- Fault Tree Analysis (FTA)


- Failure Mode Effect & Criticality Analysis (FMECA)

24


- Cause-consequence diagram


- Hazard and operability study (HAZOP)


A list of various techniques and methods used during the phases of the life-cycle is reported in Annex F.

To check/estimate the risk of systems in a mathematical way based on dependable data is not always
possible.

Initially, the analysis is performed qualitatively identifying all contributing factors and their combination
without quantification.

If explicit risk estimation is adopted, it will be possible to calculate the risk by using frequency of occurrences
and severity of its consequences (adequate to the system analysed).

Frequencies and severities can be expressed quantitatively and/or semi-quantitatively, e.g. “improbable”,
“frequent”, “light injuries”, “fatalities” (see EN 50126-1 for examples of such classes).

**8.2.4** **Expert Judgement**


If a quantitative approach to explicit risk estimation is not viable because the required information is not
available, expert judgement is required in the qualitative approach. Expert judgement may be used for the
quantitative approach as well.

EXAMPLE Not available information such as data, causal connections as well as interrelations/interactions
between the system’s constituents


In some cases the expert judgement can be the only approach if dependable data cannot be produced.

To make it a credible basis for risk assessment, expert judgement should be made as objective as possible.
This implies:

- Check / estimation should not be the opinion of a single person. Agreement among several
(independent) experts and approved knowledge enhances the confidence in an assessment.


- Experts have adequate knowledge of the area in question.


- All necessary areas of expertise (which could arrive at differing classifications) should be included in
the judgement.


- If the expert judgement is applied to estimate the frequency and consequences of hazards (or of
accidents), a clear understanding of the categories promotes a common interpretation.


- The results of expert judgement are documented. This ensures the transparency and plausibility of the
conclusions. It demonstrates the integrity and enables third parties to trace the conclusion.


- The documentation is refined if new information become available.


The documentation should include:

- the participants and respective areas of expertise.


- information like references to publications, sources, assumptions, deliberately excluded aspects with
justification, rationale of conclusion, etc.


**8.3 Risk acceptance principles and risk evaluation**


**8.3.1** **Use of Code of Practice**


Codes of Practice (CoP), when correctly applied, may be used to control one or more specific hazards. The
application of Codes of Practice may be used as a risk acceptance principle. Each Code of Practice shall
meet the following requirements:


25


- be a set of rules widely recognised in the railway domain. If this is not the case, the CoP shall be
justified and;


- be relevant for the control of the hazard in the system under consideration.


EXAMPLE 1 [For safety-related transmission over a non-trusted communication system, EN 50159 might apply as](http://dx.doi.org/10.3403/30202175U)
Code of Practice.


EXAMPLE 2 [EN 50153 Railway applications — Rolling stock — Protective provisions relating to electrical hazards.](http://dx.doi.org/10.3403/00948646U)


If one or more hazards are controlled by codes of practice fulfilling the requirements above, then the risks
associated with these hazards shall be considered acceptable. This means that these risks don’t need to
be further analysed.

The use of the codes of practice shall be registered in the hazard record as safety requirements for the
relevant hazards.

If one or more hazards are controlled by codes of practice, no other risk acceptance principles need to be
applied for these hazards.

If the risk for a particular hazard cannot be acceptably covered by the application of a Code of Practice,
additional safety measures shall be identified by applying other risk acceptance principles. This can also
occur when the related Code of Practice does not sufficiently cover the identified hazards (e.g. the Code of
Practice is not applicable to the full range of hazards).

**8.3.2** **Use of a reference system**


The system under consideration may be compared with a reference system for risk assessment. The
application of a reference system may be used as a risk acceptance principle.

The similar reference system shall satisfy the following requirements:

- it has already been proven in-use to have an acceptable safety level and would therefore still qualify
for approval;


- it has similar functions and interfaces as the system under consideration;


- it is used under similar operational conditions as the system under consideration for a sufficient period
of time and has given confidence with the range of observed hazards and accidents;


- it is used under similar environmental conditions as the system under consideration.


NOTE This approach implies that the information above was recorded for the project that introduced the reference
system and that the information has been retained.


If a reference system fulfils the requirements listed above, then for the system under consideration:

- the risks associated with the hazards covered by the reference system shall be considered as
acceptable;


- the safety requirements for the hazards covered by the reference system shall be derived from the
safety analyses or from an evaluation of safety records of the reference system;


- these safety requirements shall be registered in the hazard record as safety requirements for the
relevant hazards.


If the system under assessment deviates from the reference system, the risk evaluation shall demonstrate
that the system under assessment reaches at least the same safety level as the reference system, applying
another reference system or one of the two other risk acceptance principles. The risks associated with the
hazards covered by the reference system shall, in that case, be considered as acceptable.

If at least the same safety level as the reference system cannot be demonstrated, additional safety
measures shall be identified for the deviations, applying one of the two other risk acceptance principles.

**8.3.3** **Use of Explicit Risk Estimation**


If the hazards are not covered by one of the two risk acceptance principles of sub-clause 8.3.1 or subclause 8.3.2, risk analysis shall be performed using the risk acceptance principle of explicit risk estimation
along with risk evaluation.

Explicit risk estimation evaluates the risk arising from a hazard of a system in a particular operational
context. The aim is to estimate the risk and ensure the risk is acceptable.

The explicit risk estimation shall satisfy the following requirements:

- the methods used shall reflect correctly the system under consideration and its parameters (including
all operational modes);


- the results shall be sufficiently accurate to serve as robust decision support. Minor changes in input
assumptions or prerequisites shall not result in significantly different requirements.


The results of explicit risk estimation shall be documented.

The estimation may be done quantitatively and/or qualitatively:

- Quantitative explicit risk estimation is performed by estimating the frequency of occurrence and the
severity of an accident scenario. This shall be done for the consequences of all identified hazardous
scenarios, using data and / or expert judgment;


- Qualitative explicit risk estimation shall be performed by use of expert judgement (e.g. using a logical
argument based on system definition).


The frequencies and severities may be estimated using semi-quantitative or quantitative methods.

When explicit risk estimation principle is used, it shall:

- define the risk acceptance criteria to be used to establish the acceptability of the risk level for the
consequences of the relevant hazards;


- demonstrate that the safety measures applied are able to reduce the risk to meet the risk acceptance
criteria.


If the risk associated with one hazard or a combination of several hazards is considered acceptable, the
identified safety measures shall be registered in the hazard record.

There are different methods to derive the acceptable risk level, based on legal or other requirements.

EXAMPLE 1 Prevailing legal requirements, existing technical standards, existing safety systems or processes.
These criteria are not established by this European Standard.


Annex A describes some well-known methods to define risk acceptance criteria.

When explicit risk estimation is applied, the railway duty holders may allocate safety targets for hazards at
the railway system level.

EXAMPLE 2 See Example 1 in EN 50126-1:2017, C.4.


The quantitative safety target expressed as THR for a given Hazard should relate to specific elementary
functions without considering the number of instances of those functions in whole railway system. This will
avoid that a given technical system fulfilling a defined THR and already accepted, would no longer be
considered acceptable when the number of instances increases.

Units for these safety targets can vary. When, as a result of the risk analysis, these targets are transferred
to the technical system as Tolerable Hazard Rates (THR), a transformation shall be provided since THR
shall be expressed in unit per hour (h [-1] ).

If no THRs are provided by the railway duty holder, then either he will work together to define the
requirements with the supplier or the supplier might propose them.

**8.4.1.3** **Tolerable Hazard Rate (THR)**


The Tolerable Hazard Rate (THR) can be derived for the system under consideration through engineering
judgement, investigation and agreement between the railway duty holder and the railway supplier.

Cumulative targets shall be apportioned to the system under consideration in its specific operational and
environmental context. In particular a cumulative safety target for an accident, caused by different hazards
shall be apportioned appropriately to the various hazards causing it.

The railway duty holder can also directly assign the THR to the technical hazards as a target to be reached
by the railway supplier.

External barriers can be considered between the technical hazards and the potential accidents. The risk
reduction provided by a barrier can depend on the operational context.

NOTE To illustrate the possible dependence of the risk reduction on the operational context, the barrier “traction
cut-off when train doors are open” is effective only when the train is at standstill because it prevents the train from
moving due to a traction demand. Therefore this barrier can be considered as a barrier for other functions, but it provides
no risk reduction factor when the train is running.


The described partitioning from accident safety targets to THR is a top-down approach. Alternatively, a
bottom-up method can be followed, starting from THR and verifying that the accident safety targets are
achieved.


29


If the THR is derived from an accident safety target, the complete accident chain shall be analysed. The
acceptable risks shall be apportioned between different accident types and different functional and
operational hazards so as to meet an acceptable level of safety for the complete Railway System.

- determine quantitative safety requirements at the railway system level. The railway duty holder may

- define the system under consideration and its operational and environmental context and provide this
information to the railway supplier along with the THR associated to the identified hazards.


The railway duty holder and the railway supplier should agree on the list of hazards associated with the
system under consideration:

- hazards which are known by either party are communicated to all parties concerned to help ensure
completeness;


- the railway duty holder can make reasonable assumptions about the achievable frequency of
occurrence of hazards associated with the technical system in its target setting, risk assessments and
safety management activities;


- the railway supplier is aware of the operational context in which hazards occur, and the other hazards
outside of the technical system that might be relevant, so that railway supplier can take account of
these in the system design.


The railway supplier

- can review the assumptions and confirm their validity for the system under development.


- will demonstrate that the hazard rates estimated for the design meet the THR possibly under a number
of assumptions made to support the analysis, and in the hypothesis that all assumed mitigations are
subsequently applied.


All of the sets of assumptions, established by the railway supplier shall be documented and passed to the
integrator or railway duty holder as application conditions. These application conditions shall be accepted
and then applied by the user. For the design of a generic product, system, subsystem, hardware or software
for a wide range of applications, the specific context cannot be defined. Therefore the system designer
should make assumptions about the operational use of the system and its environment, its technical
hazards and potential accidents. The designer shall assign a THR to the technical hazards. These
assumptions are part of the application conditions for the use of the system (including operational and
maintenance conditions).

**8.4.2** **Variability using quantitative risk estimates**


**8.4.2.1** **General**


Methods used give approximate estimates of the levels of risk generated by hazards. The results obtained
from these methods should be used with an awareness of the inherent uncertainties.

At the very early stage of the project life-cycle when allocating safety requirements (phases from 1 to 3), a
high level of uncertainty in risk estimates becomes a major issue in the process of allocating safety
requirements. Dealing with this uncertainty in an appropriate manner is a vital part of the risk management
process.

The following approaches may be used:


30


- “Worst possible scenario”, 8.4.2.2, or


- “Reasonable estimates”, 8.4.2.3, or


- “Reasonable worst case”, 8.4.2.4.


Another possible approach is to analyse hazards into scenarios with greater or lesser consequences, e.g.
collision (high speed) and collision (low speed) generating different subset of safety requirements
associated to the overall hazard. This reduces the range of risk uncertainty for each subset of safety
requirements.

**8.4.2.2** **“Worst possible scenario”**


The most conservative approach for dealing with uncertainty is to take a worst possible scenario. This is
done throughout the requirement allocation process (in phase 3 of life-cycle process).

Depending on the available specific project data, the “worst possible scenario” might be applied as follows
(i.e. the scenario with the highest risk):

- It is assumed that if a hazard could lead to a number of possible accidents, the worst possible scenario
(that means: the scenario with the highest risk) should be considered;


- If the frequency at which a hazard occurs is estimated as a range based on a statistical analysis of
data, then the highest frequency of the range is used in the analysis;


- If the effect of a hazard is estimated by “expert judgement”, then the worst case scenario assessment
of any expert is taken.


**8.4.2.3** **“Reasonable estimates”**


The recommended methodology for dealing with uncertainty of data is to use reasonable estimates
throughout the risk management process, also for setting safety targets (THR at higher systems level).

These estimates can include the likelihood of a particular hazard based on the operational profile and taking
into account all factors that might avert or reduce the severity of an accident including human actions.

Estimates of frequency and consequence can be based on:

- collected data for existing railways,


- extrapolated data from similar situations on other railways or even other modes of transport, calculated
failure rates using generic failure rate prediction tools,


- expert judgement.


At the end of the process an uncertainty estimation can be made, taking into account that different
uncertainties in individual estimates can cancel out or add up.

This estimation can be used to adjust targets or change the acceptance criterion as a reasonable safety
allowance.

**8.4.2.4** **“Reasonable worst case”**


An intermediate approach between “worst possible scenario”” and “reasonable estimates” is the
“reasonable worst case”. A worst case is applied for one of the dominant factors in the safety analysis so
as to introduce a reasonable measure of conservatism. Thereafter “reasonable estimates” shall be
accepted for all other dominant factors, otherwise the whole analysis will degrade into the “worst case”
analysis.

**8.4.3** **Qualitative and semi-quantitative approaches**


In a semi-quantitative or qualitative method, explicit risk estimation is carried out using judgement to allocate
the frequency and severity into predefined categories which can be more or less numerical in nature.


31


This approach is particularly useful in the early stage of project life-cycle when little analysis or information
is available. At this stage, the categorisation of hazards is useful in order to determine their relative risks
and to focus attention.

Examples of and guidance for the calibration of accident frequency categories and severity categories and
risk tolerability matrix are shown in EN 50126-1:2017, Annex C.


**9** **Specification of System Safety Requirements**


**9.1 General**


A function, component, product, system or procedure is called safety-related if at least one of its properties
is used in the safety argument for the system in which it is applied. These properties can be of functional
or non-functional nature

Being "safety-related" means having been assigned with one or more safety requirements. This is the result
of the risk analysis and risk evaluation, comprising acceptability of control measures and residual risk.

The process of risk assessment shall result in a set of system safety requirements agreed between the
stakeholders. Safety requirements may either be contained in a separate safety requirements specification
or be tagged as “safety requirement” in the system requirements specification.

**9.2 Safety requirements**


The specification of safety requirements shall consider the following:

- safety-related functions;


- safety-related assumptions such as effectiveness (probability of failure on demand, per hour, etc.) of
mitigation barriers (e.g. protection systems, redundancies);


- tolerable hazard rates (THR) or TFFR for quantitative requirements, if defined during the explicit risk
estimation, considering:


   - definition of safe states;


   - definition of the maximum permitted time to enter a safe state;


   - failure detection measures or facilities or devices;


- requirements resulting from the hazard analysis performed at upper level;


- adaptation to interfaces;


- organisational rules;


- operational rules;


- maintenance rules;


- environmental conditions;


- legal safety requirements.


**9.3 Categorization of Safety Requirements**


**9.3.1** **General**


Safety requirements can be categorized as follows:

- functional safety requirements (9.3.2);


32


- technical safety requirements (9.3.3);


- contextual safety requirements (9.3.4).


**Figure 9 — requirements classification**


**9.3.2** **Functional safety requirements**


After performing the risk analysis, a function can be identified as “safety-related” and assigned with
functional safety requirements.

NOTE It can happen that a function, initially considered as possibly safety-related, at the end of the Risk Analysis
is not given any safety requirement: for instance because the related hazard is mitigated by other means or because it
is considered broadly acceptable. In this case the function will be considered as non-safety-related.


If no property of the function is used to achieve and demonstrate safety, and no safety requirements are
given, the function is not safety-related and no additional measures for safety approval are necessary.

Functional safety requirements shall include:

- the expected functional behaviour of safety-related functions;


- the behaviour of the safety-related functions in case of failures, divided into:


a) the required safety integrity requirements;


b) the required behaviour in case of non-hazardous failure (i.e. enforcement and retention of safe state).


Safety integrity relates to the ability of a safety-related system to achieve its required safety functions. The
higher the safety integrity, the lower the likelihood that it will fail to carry out the required safety functions.

Safety Integrity of the function can be affected both by random and systematic failures; therefore safety
integrity comprises two parts: systematic and random safety integrity.

Prevention of systematic and random failures can require different approaches:

- the Random safety integrity is achieved by product design (e.g. diversity, redundancy, protection
against expected environmental conditions foreseen in specific Codes of Practice etc.);


33


- the Systematic safety integrity can benefit from technical mechanisms embedded in a product (for
instance diversity), but it is mainly based on process solutions, as quality management, safety
management, and organizational measures.


Replacement actions could be sufficient in case of random causes. However, in the case of failures due to
systematic errors, replacing the faulty item can only provide a first response and further corrective actions
could be required.

When well-defined failure models and common data-bases are available, a quantitative assessment can
be carried out for the random failure aspect of safety integrity.

There is currently no commonly accepted basis for quantifying systematic failures. Therefore, the methods
defined in this standard preclude quantification of systematic failures.

**9.3.3** **Technical safety requirements**


Technical safety requirements are linked to the technical design and implementation of the system.

Technical requirements for each subsystem/product can be derived from different aspects such as
maintainability, environmental conditions, potential threats created by the technology/system/subsystem
regardless of their intended functions.

EXAMPLES Fire hazards, interior hazards such as sharp edges, presence of electric voltage, presence of
combustible material, hazards related to structural integrity, presence of harmful agents/substances, mechanical
strength, unsafe behaviour under physical conditions such as moisture, heat, fire, etc.


Technical safety requirements comprise technical constraints for design/installation/use such as insulation
coordination, earthing and electromagnetic compatibility. They can include safety requirements such as:

- conformity to external standards,


- relevant regulations,


- Codes of Practice.


**9.3.4** **Contextual safety requirements**


Contextual safety requirements cover operational and maintenance safety requirements.

Operators should ensure the implementation of operational safety requirements, including:

- specific actions expected for any category of personnel concerned;


- the expected operational procedures for normal and abnormal operation modes;


- assumptions about safety-related operational restrictions;


EXAMPLE 1 Speed, number of trains operating, average operating time, signalling instruction.


- human interface rules.


EXAMPLE 2 Training demand, demand on staff, set of available competences of operational staff.


Maintenance safety requirements consist of a list of safety-related maintenance actions such as:

- maintenance


   - intervals;


   - rules;


   - procedures for specific applications;


34


   - set of tests and safety checks before returning a system to service after maintenance;


- limitations


   - of spare parts storage condition;


   - on type of tools used;


   - on physical characteristics of tools to be used.

a. any of them is able to counteract the hazard at this level, regardless of the presence or absence of the

from sub-functions (e.g.: detection and negation requirements).

Regarding common cause of failures and the various types of independence, the following apply:

- freedom from common random failures that jeopardize safety.


   - It implies that, at a given architectural level of implementation, the set of items building up this
architecture do not fail in a critical way, simultaneously or in cascade, as a consequence of a
common physical cause.


   - Any physical connection, interaction or common physical influence (shared physical resources,
energy flowing, common environment) can lead to a common physical cause that jeopardize
safety.


- freedom from common systematic failures that jeopardize safety.


   - It implies that no process-related causes prevent the identification of flaws inheriting the potential
for common cause failures. Organisational independence (see Clause 7) and diversity in human
resource and life-cycle processes are deemed to contribute to higher overall safety integrity for
products and systems.


   - Any common process activities in the design, manufacturing, installation and maintenance phases
(e.g. same human resource, common modelling, etc.) can be a cause of common mistakes during
the development phase of the two functions.


- freedom from both common random failures and common systematic failures that jeopardize safety.

**10.2.3 Safety Integrity Factors**


Safety Integrity of the function in general expresses the confidence placed into achievement and
maintenance of the safety requirements.

Safety Integrity can be affected by random and systematic failures. It expresses the level of confidence that
the achievement of the safety requirement is not corrupted by both.

Environmental conditions should be included within systematic aspects of safety integrity as appropriate.

**10.2.4 Functional safety integrity and random failures**


When a Tolerable Functional unsafe Failure Rate (TFFR) is defined, all the other relevant quantitative and
qualitative requirements for each function shall be set, in order to fulfill the THR target defined at the hazard
level.

In fact, the TFFR is not sufficient to characterise a safety-related function. The necessary functional failure
model and the architectural design shall address:

- the functional unsafe failures;


- how the system is able to detect the fault if required;


- the safety design principles that assure a safe reaction;


- the safe down time (SDT, see Annex D.3.1) if needed in the model;


- how the function exits the fault state, e.g. by restoration or entering in a safe state;


- how the system contains the fault effects (system recovery, emergency procedures);


- the procedural and maintenance actions to be implemented and what is the periodicity of those actions
to be implemented.


Auxiliary functions (diagnostics) and preventive maintenance should also be taken in account.

**10.2.5 Systematic aspect of functional safety integrity**


Quality and safety processes have the objective to prevent and reduce systematic design faults. This should
include human errors in all phases, not only in the design phase.

An effective safety management is essential to minimise systematic faults during all the phases of the lifecycle.

which the sub-function belongs.

common systematic and random failures of items is required.

the SIL qualitative measures and therefore are not contributing to the calculation and quantitative fulfilment

**10.2.12 Prevention of misuse of SILs**


This subclause provides some warnings about the use of SIL for electronic systems.

NOTE Sometimes the SIL concept is misunderstood and used for non-intended applications.


The following non-comprehensive list gives advice on how SILs should not be used:

- SILs should be assigned at the level of the last independent function only after a risk analysis in the
appropriate life-cycle phases according to one of the risk acceptance principles or equivalent
derivation. It is meaningless to assign SILs prior to completing such an analysis;


- SILs should not be used for non-functional safety, e.g. applying SILs to safety against slips, trips and
falls;


- Fulfilling all quantitative and qualitative integrity requirements does not guarantee that the related
function is correctly defined;


- SILs should not be used for describing systems attributes, e.g. “this is a SIL 4 computer”. The correct
wording would be: "this is a computer capable of performing the specified SIL 4 safety-related
functions, provided that all SRAC associated to this computer are applied".

**11 Design and implementation**


**11.1 Introduction**


This subclause provides guidance on phases 5 and 6 of the lifecycle (see EN 50126-1:2017, 7.6 and 7.7).

During these phases, hazard analysis shall be performed, comprising causal analysis, refined hazard
identification and common-cause analysis.

**11.2 Causal analysis**


Causal analysis is the systematic investigation of the hierarchy of causes deriving from the technical
solution. The aim is to identify the logic sequences of hazardous events that can lead to an undesirable
effect.

During the causal analysis the following shall be identified:

- any function, system or subsystem failure, credible human error and/or credible external factors
(permanent or not) that can cause technical hazard,


- all operating rules and safety barriers that could fail in protecting from the hazard.


The causal analysis consists of:

- identification of all causal factors: environmental, functional, interface, hardware, software, human;


- identification of logical relationship between cause and effect in consideration of the hazards at the
boundary of the system under consideration;


- identification of the combination of events or mechanisms linking cause to the effect (i.e. hazard);


- identification and modelling of common cause failures by applying common cause analysis.


The causal analysis techniques aim to identify the logic sequence of the events developing into a hazard.
The analysis may be performed either qualitatively or quantitatively, using a top-down approach. For
complex scenarios, investigation of the logic cause-effect sequence is completed by using a combination

43


of bottom-up techniques or mixed bottom-up and top-down techniques. Typical causal analysis techniques
are:

- Fault Tree Analysis (FTA);


- FMECA;


- Markov analysis;


- Event Tree Analysis (ETA);


- Human factor analysis.


**11.3 Hazard identification (refinement)**


Realisation of a system can lead to unforeseen or undesirable properties with a potential to cause harm to
people, in particular if the system or technology is new. Unforeseen hazards can arise because of several
aspects:

- new technology has a potential for new hazards, that could not be immediately identified due to a lack
of experience or knowledge;


- emergence of additional hazards in the existing system due to a technological transfer (e.g. from
analogue to digital technology);


- mistakes in new design due to a lack of adequate/proper specification;


- special operation modes in an existing system might not fit well and can create new hazards associated
to actions performed by operators, maintainers or other members of the staff, public, etc.;


- design errors can create new hazards but they can often be related to already identified ones.


These aspects can give rise to potential causes of hazards at the level of the system under consideration,
which require the same systematic treatment as applied to the already identified hazards at the railway
system level. Then it is possible to proceed in at least two different ways:

- if the hazard at the level of the system under consideration can be considered as a new cause of an
already identified hazard, the railway supplier should make sure that the resulting hazard rate,
considering also the new causes, is still compliant with the THR that has been fixed by the railway duty
holder;


- If the hazard at the level of the system under consideration leads to a new hazard, or exceeds the
THR, the railway supplier shall communicate it to the railway duty holder and provide all the information
from the hazard analysis (causes, consequences, risk). The railway duty holder should then analyse
these new conditions. Depending on the perceived risks, a qualitative or quantitative assessment is
required, with a view to forecast and agreement on an appropriate tolerable level (THR). These might
lead to updated requirements.


For both cases all the actions shall be recorded in the hazard log and referred to in the safety case.

**A.2 ALARP (As Low As Reasonably Practicable)**


**A.2.1 General**


ALARP means “As Low As Reasonably Practicable” and refers to a legal duty to reduce risk. The ALARP
principle originally arose as a legal requirement in the UK to reduce the risks arising from work activities
“so far as is reasonably practicable”, sometimes abbreviated as SFAIRP. It imposes a relative duty, stating
that the railway duty holder should apply any safety measures that will reduce risk ALARP. The judgement
of whether or not a particular control is ALARP is based on a comparison of the net costs, taking account
of any commercial benefits, with the extent of risk reduction of any safety measure/safety requirement under
consideration.

In reality, the application of established good practice, including formal codes of practice, can often be
considered to be a suitable demonstration that risk is reduced ALARP. However in some cases there is a
need to undertake the analysis of costs and benefits more formally, for example via quantified risk
assessment and Cost Benefit Analysis (CBA). Clear guidance on how to make ALARP judgements and
undertake ALARP CBA is included in the publication ‘Taking Safe Decisions’ (RSSB, 2008,
[http://www.rssb.co.uk/).](http://www.rssb.co.uk/)

NOTE An example of guidance on ALARP judgements and CBA can be found in the bibliography entry for ‘Taking
safe decisions.


Important points to note are that:

- The ALARP principle considers the change in risk and the net cost of a control measure. Therefore the
risk being considered could relate to a range of hazards, affecting a range of people to some extent.
ALARP therefore uses collective risk estimates, and is applied to control measures;


- The ALARP principle does not take into account absolute risk levels or considerations of the tolerability
of risk. It is purely based on a comparison of the costs of a measure with the risk reduction it achieves;


47


- The ALARP principle is the main criterion for establishing whether or not a safety measure is required.
However there are alternative criteria which can be usefully considered to support judgements. These
are:


   - The residual level of risk on a railway, which can be used as a benchmark of the acceptability of
an absolute level of risk calculated;


   - The totality of fatality risk to which a particular category of individual is exposed. This can be set
by the regulator – and is unlikely to be relevant when all ALARP measures have been applied on
a railway project.


**A.2.2 Tolerability and ALARP**


A risk could be deemed to be tolerable, but if there are reasonably practicable measures which can be
adopted to achieve further reduction in the risk it cannot be considered to be ALARP until these measures
have been adopted.

The ALARP principle is not generally applied to risks that are comparable to those that people regard as
insignificant or trivial in their daily lives, typically risks from activities that are inherently not very hazardous
or from hazardous activities that can be, and are, readily controlled to produce very low risks. In such
instances it would be disproportionate to seek further reduction in risk.

It is conceivable that a risk might be both ALARP and intolerable, i.e. even when everything that is
reasonably practicable has been done the residual risk remains at an intolerable level. In such a case the
activity responsible for generating the risk should not be permitted.


**A.3 Globalement Au Moins Equivalent (GAME) principle**


**A.3.1 Principle**


The GAME approach is legally required in France. Some other countries use parts of the application
described as well.

The principle is:

- any new guided public transport system/subsystem or


- any modification of an existing system/subsystem


should be globally as safe or safer than the existing accepted as a reference system.

To achieve this level, the system or modification should be designed and carried out so that the overall
level of safety for users, operating staff and third party [3)] is at least equivalent to the safety level of existing
systems providing similar services.

This takes into account the experience of the past and requires that the safety performance of the projected
system is not worse than those of similar systems (reference), by the phrase “at least”. It does not consider
safety at a particular risk level, but at the global safety level of the studied system/subsystem, by using the
requirement “globally”. The transport system supplier is free to allocate risk portions to subsystems within
the system or between “similar” risks and apply the relevant approach, i.e. qualitative or quantitative
(depending of the type of failure that leads to the hazard). For example, a quantitative analysis could be
useful to evaluate the failure rate of an equipment item but might not be relevant for a hazard due to a
human factor.

When applying GAME principle both collective and individual risks should be taken into consideration.

**A.3.2 Using GAME**


**A.3.2.1 General**


GAME principle can be used in different ways for different purposes. This subclause explains the different
ways in which GAME can be an effective and efficient approach to assess the acceptability of risk
associated with a certain system.

**A.3.2.2 Basic principles**


There are a few important prerequisites for applying GAME:

- the system under consideration can be compared to an equivalent or similar (with respect to
application) reference system;


- a clear system boundary can be defined for both new and reference systems;


- the properties relevant to the risks considered are known for both the new as well as the reference
systems;


- any differences in properties need to be compensated in the setting of risk targets or in demonstration
of compliance;


- the proof that the reference system is considered acceptable in respect of safety (otherwise, this
system cannot be taken as reference).


The GAME demonstration should be done through:

- a systematic approach to safety that allows the identification of hazards for the overall system and the
definition of the relevant requirements;


- the proof that each hazard is prevented by barriers or lessened by mitigations;


- the proof that each barrier and mitigation is effective regarding the corresponding hazard;


- a relevant safety management process (based on recognized and relevant standard) intended to
record, manage and control the hazards (and their corresponding barriers or mitigations) and the
relevant requirements.


**A.3.2.3 Using GAME to construct a qualitative safety argument**


In some cases a qualitative argument can be used to demonstrate compliance using the GAME principle.
If GAME is used in this way it is very important to establish and demonstrate that the application conditions
for both systems are identical. Any differences in application conditions need to be scrutinized for a potential
to:

- introduce new hazards;


- affect the probability of occurrence of known hazards;


- extend the consequences of known hazards.


**A.3.2.4 GAME using quantitative risk targets**


Another way of applying GAME is by using quantitative risk targets. This can be done on any integration
level in the railway system (both for a complete railway system and for a subsystem). The reference will
always be the contribution from the system to the risk at the level of the complete railway system.

**A.4 Minimum Endogenous Mortality MEM**


MEM is a method to derive absolute values for risk acceptance based on the natural death rate of human
beings of specified age.

MEM is an approach with predefined assumptions and is based on the work of A. Kuhlmann in Germany in
1981. The following paragraphs give a summary of that paper.

The MEM criterion allocates the same risk to an individual, independent of any technical system. The
allocation of the same risk to an individual needs to be justified if MEM criterion is used.

MEM incorporates the lowest natural death rate and uses this to assure that the total additional technical
risk for an individual does not exceed a value equivalent to this natural risk. The natural death rate is
focusing only on natural causes of death without any kind of accidents and native malformation influences.

In the range between 5 years and 15 years of age for humans, the natural death rate (Rm) in industrial
developed states reaches a minimum for human individuals:

Rm = 2 x 10 [-4] fatalities / (person x year)

The MEM requirement states that the additional overall hazard death rate caused by technical systems (Rt)
should not exceed this limit:

Rt ≤ Rm

and each single system should not contribute more than 5 % because each individual is endangered by
n different technical systems in parallel; the assumption in the MEM principle is:

n ≤ 20

It means that a single technical system should not lead to a risk of fatality (R) of a single person with a rate
of:

R ≤ 10 [-5] fatality / (person x year)

THRs cannot be calculated from accident statistics unless rigorously collected statistics and models are
available. However, even if such models do not exist the use of such statistics, when present, can be a part
of the process of reaching an informed judgement of the level at which to set THRs.

Railway network statistics and data can be used to determine sensible THRs in some circumstances. This
can be done, as is current practice in the UK, by:

- Reviewing the frequency of occurrence of functional/technical hazards across the railway network;


- Using this information, and information on the number of units installed across a given scope of railway
to calculate average hazardous failure rates per unit in that given area (e.g. national rail network, or
part of it);


- Reviewing accident and incident rates to estimate the residual risk on the network from such functional
hazards and, by implication, whether the current functional hazard rates are acceptable.


The hazardous failure rate for a given railway system per operational hour could be estimated by:

λhour per unit = Nyear /(hoursyear* number of units).

with Nyear as the number of hazardous failures per year.

For example if we assume that:

- a national railway has 60 000 track circuits on its network;


- on average 6 of these fail ‘wrongside’ i.e. in such a way that they do not indicate when they are
occupied, in any given year;


- each track circuit is operational for 19 hours per day, 365 days per year (6 935 operational hours per
year).


Then:

λhour per unit =6/(6 935*60 000) = 1,4E-08 failures per hour.

However to understand whether or not this functional hazard rate is acceptable for adoption as a THR there
needs to be some understanding if the residual risk associated with it is acceptable. This would be based
on a review of the accident statistics over a number of years to determine whether a hazard of this type
had led to any accidents. If over a reasonable period the hazard had resulted in no accidents then there
could be a conclusion that the rate of failure was acceptable, given other risk control measures and
circumstantial factors. If accidents had occurred, analysis of the hazard and other barriers relevant to their
occurrence should form part of the judgement.

This calculation does not need to include a mean time to repair, if it is assumed that each failure would be
detected when the next train arrives at the track circuit. So it could be assumed that, for every failure there
is a similar chance of an accident.

If the THR is to be used for a new or modified railway system, an impact analysis regarding the
environmental and functional contexts between the existing network and the new or modified railway
system should be led to check the applicability of the determined THR for the system whose failure could
lead to the hazard and the resulting accident.

**Guidance on SIL Allocation**


This annex provides guidance on SIL Allocation with a list of typical questions about the use and misuse of
SIL allocation criteria.

1. Is it correct to say that SIL is related to the systematic part of the safety integrity, and TFFR is related
to the random part?


This is not correct. Whilst it is correct to state that TFFR is related mainly to the random part of the
safety integrity, the associated SIL provides a link with the systematic part. SIL defines a set of
qualitative measures. Many of them aim at protecting against systematic failures, but some are
defences against random failures (e.g., specific architectures or the single-fault principle)


2. For a single function, are the SIL qualitative measures to be applied also to sub-functions in the
functional decomposition?


A part of the requirements expressed for the function apply all over the development, therefore are
also applicable to its constituents (e.g. quality and safety measures). Some requirements, such as
architectural safety constraints, are applicable at the level of the function only.


3. Can two functions, related to different hazards, share the same SIL qualitative measures? For instance,
same design features, same dynamic fault detection, etc.


Yes they can. Safety-related functions within a system are implemented through a development
process and by sub-systems. The process fulfils quality and safety measures, and the sub-system
implements the architectural measures – measures defined within the set of the respective SIL
qualitative measures.


Both the process and the sub-system will be common to a number of different functions.


4. What if a sub-system implements a number of functions, related to different hazards, each of which
could require different SIL?


Where this is the case, two alternative options are possible:


   - every function meets the highest SIL demanded;


   - if demonstration of mutual non-intrusiveness can be provided, every function can satisfy its own
required SIL.


5. Can the SIL of a function be apportioned to its sub-functions?


This is possible only for TFFR under the conditions for independence. When a function is divided into
a number of sub-functions, this apportionment should not be reflected in an apportionment of the SIL
itself – as the SIL is assigned only once during the apportionment. Then, during its design and
implementation, if mechanisms exist to prevent the failure of a component from causing the function
to go to an unsafe state, the allocation can be redone and come to the conclusion that a less stringent
SIL could be sufficient.


6. How can SIL be allocated for an electronic part of a complex function made with various technologies?


In this case, no general rule can be given.


53


Consider for instance a door locking system. To keep the door locked, safety is guaranteed both by a
mechanical lock and an electronic control. The mechanical part will be built according to a CoP,
therefore the hazards are sufficiently dealt with according to the CoP approach. However, SIL for the
electronic control could also be higher if the control is needed for other safety-related functions.

**D.1 Analysis of the system and methods**


The goal of apportioning system safety requirements is to “break down” the safety integrity targets
determined at the level of a system to the lower levels, i.e. onto sub-functions and sub-systems, and when

rate” simplification.

Calculating a failure rate for a system using only the failure rates of the components (thus not using any
safe down time) will generally largely overestimate the safety of the system:

**E.3 Using formulas out of their range of applicability**
